Since the full-scale conflict in Ukraine began in 2022, Ukraine’s military has been operating in an environment defined by sophisticated missile threats and electronic warfare countermeasures.

Ukraine’s operational experience in employing Western ground-based air defence systems provides invaluable lessons in achieving both high levels of ballistic and drone interception capability and cost-effectiveness, even when facing aggressive and adaptive adversaries.

Modern western ground-based air defence systems – principally the American Patriot, the Franco-Italian SAMP/T, the Norwegian NASAMS, and the German IRIS-T - have been critical in defending its critical assets against advanced systems deployed by a near-peer adversary.

**Performance**

Measuring performance with high-information metrics - 100% interception of key hypersonic threats, nearly 100% success against cruise missiles, and improved success rates against drones - demonstrates that with rapid redeployment, thorough integration of EW countermeasures, and strategic cost management, ground-based air defense can be both resilient and effective.

Ukraine’s integration of sophisticated air defense technologies has provided insights into their operational performance and practical capabilities amid a high-intensity conflict:

- **100% **confirmed interception by the Patriot system against Kinzhal hypersonic missiles, with an average reaction time of 8 seconds.

- **Near 100%** success against Kalibr cruise missiles by the IRIS-T SLM system, bolstered by recent software enhancements.

- **94%** hit rate by NASAMS systems. Approximately 900 interceptions. Around 60% of the targets were cruise missiles, including the Kh-101, Kh-555, Kh-59, Kh-69 and Kalibr.

**Mobility, dispersion and deception**

Ukrainian forces transformed air defense from static emplacements into dynamic systems through mobile units and shoot-and-scoot tactics, enabling rapid repositioning to evade counterstrikes. Camouflaging systems within terrain and urban environments further enhanced survivability against a numerically superior adversary. This mobility disrupts traditional aerial assault patterns, forcing attackers to contend with unpredictable defensive positions. 

Early dispersion of air defense assets and decentralized command structures reduced vulnerability to initial strikes. By avoiding concentration, Ukraine maintained operational resilience despite Russia’s numerical advantage. This principle is reinforced by distributed logistics, ensuring continued missile resupply even under attrition.

**Adaption and innovation**

Facing resource constraints, Ukraine repurposed Soviet-era weapons into novel systems, such as ground-launched R-73 air-to-air missiles(originally for fighter jets) to counter drones and cruise missiles. These "FrankenSAM" hybrids demonstrate how legacy systems can be modernized with new platforms, leveraging infrared guidance for fire-and-forget capabilities. Such innovation extends to integrating commercial drones for reconnaissance and targeting, enabling precision strikes on enemy air defenses.

**Jointness**

Effective air defense now requires tight coordination with other domains. Ukrainian F-16s not only intercept missiles but also enable deeper strikes into enemy territory, illustrating how aircraft complement ground systems. Similarly, artillery and drones compensate where air superiority is contested, creating a multi-domain defensive web.

**In conlusion**

Ukraine’s experience confirm the benefits and effectiveness of air defence as a fluid, networked endeavor where adaptability and innovation are as vital as technological sophistication. These principles - mobility, layered integration, resourceful modernization, and joint-domain synergy - provide a blueprint for countering advanced aerial threats in contested environments.
